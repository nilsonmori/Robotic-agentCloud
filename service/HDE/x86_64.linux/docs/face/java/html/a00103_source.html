1 Eventual Consistency {#DCPS_Eventual_Consistency}
2 ====================
3 
4 Eventual Consistency is an important property for distributed systems:
5 it guarantees that all subscribers to the same topic will eventually
6 converge to the same end-state. Of course not every \ref DCPS_Modules_Subscription "Subscriber" will
7 see the exact same state at every single moment in time (one subscriber
8 could have lost a sample during transmission and will need to wait for
9 its retransmission, while others could have received it successfully
10 already), but eventually all Subscribers will end up with the same end
11 state for the same instances.
12 
13 Why is this such an important property for distributed systems? You
14 could argue that if each instance is updated periodically and some
15 Subscribers miss out on a sample, then they will eventually re-align
16 with the rest of the system upon reception of the next update for that
17 same instance. And that is indeed true for periodic data, but what if the
18 data is a-periodic in nature, or has a very long update period? Then one
19 or more Subscribers may feed their processing algorithms with different
20 input than similar Subscribers on other nodes and therefore reach a different
21 end state when compared to the others: their state is in that case said to be
22 not consistent with the state of the other Subscribers until the successful
23 reception of the next update for the same instance, which depending on the
24 update frequency of the Writer, may be for a long time to come.
25 
26 It depends on the nature and type of your system whether it is a problem
27 if one or more Subscribers have a different view of the world than others
28 for a potentially long interval of time. In many systems however this is
29 undesired behavior, and it is the reason why several middleware products
30 offer things like reliable transmission protocols, and in case of DDS, why
31 we offer policies like durability: the idea is that any Subscriber no matter
32 where or when it is started, has the ability to obtain the same instance state
33 as all the other Subscribers to the same Topic in the same system.
34 
35 This section will give some more background on which factors come into play
36 when you want your system to behave in an eventually consistent way and in
37 the exact mechanisms used to guarantee eventual consistency.
38 
39 ##Configuring for Eventual Consistency
40 The settings for the following QosPolicies play an important role in determining
41 whether eventual consistency can be guaranteed:
42 - \ref DCPS_QoS_Reliability "Reliability"
43 - \ref DCPS_QoS_Durability "Durability"
44 - \ref DCPS_QoS_DestinationOrder "Destination Order"
45 
46 Let's talk about each one of these and their impact on eventual consistency.
47 
48 ###Reliability:
49 When a system needs to have Eventual Consistency, it either needs to have high
50 frequency periodic updates for all its instances, or it will need to use a
51 reliable protocol. Otherwise samples may be lost during transmission and will
52 cause the system not to be eventually consistent.
53 Please note that our reliable protocol is order preserving, which means it
54 will never deliver samples from the same source out of order. However, samples
55 from different sources may still be received out of order due to non-deterministic
56 latencies on the network.
57 
58 ###Durability:
59 When a system needs to have Eventual Consistency, it either needs to have high
60 frequency periodic updates for all its instances, or it will need to use either
61 TRANSIENT or PERSISTENT durability. Otherwise late joining Subscribers will not
62 be able to converge to the same instances state as the ones that were already
63 running when the data was actually published.
64 
65 ###Destination Order:
66 When a system needs to have Eventual Consistency, it will have to use a
67 BY_SOURCE_TIMESTAMP destination order and preferably synchronized clocks.
68 The latter becomes important when the same instance may be updated by multiple
69 Writers running on different nodes.
70 
71 The effects of DestinationOrderQosPolicy especially matter when consecutive
72 updates are received out of order. The most common way in which is may happen is
73 if the consecutive updates originate from different Publishers and have different
74 latencies. If the latency of the first sample is longer than that of the second
75 sample but the second sample is published sooner than the difference in latencies,
76 then out of order delivery is a fact. But out of order delivery is even possible
77 when both samples originate from the same Publisher, even though the reliable protocol
78 guarantees no out of order delivery. An example of the latter case is a late joining
79 Transient DataReader who is requesting initial data from the durability service,
80 but who already receives a newer update directly from the originating \ref DCPS_Modules_Publication_DataWriter "DataWriter"
81 before the durability service was able to service his request.
82 
83 So why does BY_RECEPTION_TIMESTAMP not guarantee eventual consistency in case of
84 out of order delivery? Consider the scenario where two Publishers are updating the
85 same instance: Publisher A sends its update at t1, Publisher B sends the update at
86 t2 where t2 > t1. So most Subscribers will receive the update from Publisher B last,
87 and agree that this represents the latest state. However, some Subscribers may have
88 received the update from Publisher A last (for example because they were late joiners
89 and the durability service sent the A sample after the B sample has been received
90 directly from Publisher B, or because Publisher A's sample was lost during transmission
91 and its retransmit arrived after the succesful delivery of Publisher's B message) and
92 will therefore conclude that Publisher's A sample represents the latest state. The
93 effect of this is that both groups of Subscribers are not guaranteed to be eventual
94 consistent.
95 
96 So how does BY_SOURCE_TIMESTAMP guarantee eventual consistency in that case? Let's take the previous
97 example again and look at the consequences of the out of order delivery of the samples published
98 at t1 and t2. The latter sample arrived first, and it will be inserted into the Reader cache upon
99 arrival. Now when the sample with t1 arrives later, one of three things may happen:
100 -# If the t2 sample is still there and there is enough history depth left (KEEP_LAST with depth > 1
101 or KEEP_ALL with MAX_SAMPLES_PER_INSTANCE > 1), the t1 sample will be back inserted into the history
102 so that t2 will remain the latest state.
103 -# If the t2 sample is still there and there is not enough history depth left (for example KEEP_LAST with
104 depth = 1 or KEEP_ALL with MAX_SAMPLES_PER_INSTANCE = 1) then the t1 sample will be dropped so that t2 will
105 remain the latest state. In case of a KEEP_LAST policy this is not considered an issue, since only the latest
106 'depth' samples need to be stored, but in case of KEEP_ALL this violates the expectation that no samples
107 will ever be lost. So why choose this approach then? The answer is simple: delivering the sample in this case
108 would violate the resource limits that may not be exceeded. So in this case there is a direct conflict
109 between two orthogonal policies, and by dropping the sample we choose to satisfy the \ref DCPS_QoS_ResourceLimits "ResourceLimitsQosPolicy"
110 over the \ref DCPS_QoS_History "HistoryQosPolicy". However, the \ref DCPS_Modules_Subscription_DataReader "DataReader" will be informed about the dropped sample by notifying
111 the \ref DCPS_Status_SampleLost "SampleLostStatus", which may be picked up by the DataReader's Listener or its StatusCondition.
112 -# If the t2 sample has already been consumed (i.e. taken out of the DataReader), then the t1 sample
113 can no longer be inserted even though there would be enough resources in the history to store it,
114 as this would violate the eventual consistency. After all, how could the reading application know that the
115 t1 sample would not represent the latest known state in that case? For that reason each instance keeps track
116 of the source timestamp of the latest sample consumed: any incoming sample with a source timestamp older
117 than the latest one consumed will be dropped, even in case of a KEEP_ALL policy, as delivering it would
118 break eventual consistency. However, the DataReader will be informed about the dropped sample by notifying
119 its \ref DCPS_Status_SampleLost "SampleLostStatus", which may be picked up by the DataReader's Listener or its StatusCondition.
120 
121 ##Eventual Consistency in case of a disconnect/reconnect cycle.
122 Now what happens to a system when one part is temporarily disconnected from another
123 part? During this disconnect period both parts will basically continue to operate independently
124 and their states will slowly start to diverge when new updates can't flow from one part of
125 the system to the other part. Of course Eventual Consistency cannot be maintained in a system
126 that is physically disconnected (we call this the split-brain syndrome), but what happens when
127 the two parts are reconnected after they have diverged already? Can Eventual Consistency be
128 restored in these cases as well?
129 
130 For the answer to the above question we need to dive a little bit deeper into the mechanics
131 of the instance state machine and of the merge policies configured for the durability service.
132 The next section will explain the consequences of a disconnect for your instance state, and the
133 section after that will explain the consequences of a re-connect for the same instance state.
134 
135 ###Visible effects of a disconnect.
136 A DataReader may become physically disconnected from the DataWriter that conceived a number
137 of the topic instances stored in its history. In such a case the DataReader acts as if the
138 disconnected DataWriter unregistered all its instances. That might impact the state of these
139 topic instances in the following way:
140 - If the \ref DCPS_Modules_Publication_DataWriter "DataWriter" has set its \ref DCPS_QoS_WriterDataLifecycle "DataWriterLifecycleQosPolicy" field auto_dispose_unregistered_instances
141 to TRUE (the default setting), then the instance state of the concerned instances will go to
142 NOT_ALIVE_DISPOSED, no matter how many other (currently connected) DataWriters have still registered
143 the same instances.
144 - If the DataWriter has set its \ref DCPS_QoS_WriterDataLifecycle "DataWriterLifecycleQosPolicy" field auto_dispose_unregistered_instances
145 to FALSE, and no other (currently connected) DataWriters have still registered the concerned instances,
146 then the instance state of those instance will go to NOT_ALIVE_NO_WRITERS.
147 - If the DataWriter has set its \ref DCPS_QoS_WriterDataLifecycle "DataWriterLifecycleQosPolicy" field auto_dispose_unregistered_instances
148 to FALSE, but one or more (currently connected) DataWriters still have registered the concerned instances,
149 then their instance state will not be impacted and remain ALIVE.
150 
151 Please note that in all cases mentioned above the disconnection of the DataWriter will also be
152 reflected in both the \ref DCPS_Status_LivelinessChanged "LivelinessChangedStatus" and the \ref DCPS_Status_SubscriptionMatched "SubscriptionMatchedStatus".
153 
154 For Transient/Persistent data that is stored by the durability service, the same instance transitions
155 will occur. However, keep in mind that when instances are both disposed and unregistered, the durability
156 service will purge their samples after expiry of the service_cleanup_delay (0 seconds by default). That
157 means that if data needs outlive the lifespan of its originating DataWriter, such a DataWriter should set
158 its \ref DCPS_QoS_WriterDataLifecycle "DataWriterLifecycleQosPolicy" field auto_dispose_unregistered_instances to FALSE.
159 
160 ###Visible effects of a re-connect.
161 If a formerly disconnected DataWriter is re-connected to a DataReader, the state change caused by its
162 disconnect needs to be reverted. That means that the instance state should go back to ALIVE for all concerned
163 instances. However, at the same time we cannot undo the past, and just remove the DISPOSE/UNREGISTER sample
164 that caused the disconnect, for example because it, and/or the samples before it, have already been taken
165 by the application. Also, the disposed_generation_count or the no_writers_generation_count (depending on whether
166 the disconnect caused a DISPOSE or NO_WRITERS state) should be increased by one when the instance becomes ALIVE
167 again. So instead of selectively removing history, we just re-insert historical samples with the correct states
168 behind the sample that communicated the disconnect, despite the fact that according to their timestamps and
169 the \ref DCPS_QoS_DestinationOrder "DestinationOrderQosPolicy" the historical samples normally would need to be back-inserted into history before
170 the sample that communicated the disconnect. In the default case (KEEP_ALL with depth = 1) the newly inserted
171 historical sample will just push out the original historical sample, and so you might not notice the fact that
172 normal ordering is not applied here, but in case of KEEP_ALL or depth > 1, you might see the same sample with
173 the same timestamp listed twice: once before the disconnect with generation_count = n, and one after the disconnect
174 with generation_count = n + 1. The first occurrence of the sample may have already been set to READ_SAMPLE_STATE,
175 but the second occurrence of the same sample will always start with a NOT_READ_SAMPLE_STATE, and will set the
176 instance state back to NEW_INSTANCE_STATE.
177 
178 Let's look at an example:
179 At time t1 a sample is being received by a DataReader, and at t2 that DataReader disconnects from the sample's
180 originating DataWriter. At time t3 the DataReader reconnects to that DataWriter. What would the history timeline
181 look like in that case if the history depth of our DataReader would be 3? In our timeline S(t1) represents a sample
182 written at t1, and D(t2) a dispose message at t2, and NW(t2) a NO_WRITERS message at t2.
183 - For auto_dispose_unregistered_instances = TRUE, the timeline would look like this: S(t1), D(t2), S(t1).
184 - For auto_dispose_unregistered_instances = FALSE, the timeline would look like this: S(t1), NW(t2), S(t1).
185 
186 Note that in the above example, if your DataReader has KEEP_LAST with depth = 1, you will eventually see only the
187 last sample in the timeline. If your DataReader uses a depth > 1 or KEEP_ALL with max_samples_per_instance > 1 you
188 may see all three samples at the same time.
